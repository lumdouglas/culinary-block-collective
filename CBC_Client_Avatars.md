### A Day in Sarah's Life

Sarah manages everything nobody else owns. Before 9 AM she's already handling a broken HVAC, a guest wifi outage, and a Slack from the CEO asking what the all-hands lunch plan is — for next Tuesday. Catering isn't her only job. It's one of 40. She values vendors who make her look competent without requiring hand-holding.

She books catering 6–12 times per year for recurring events (monthly all-hands, weekly team lunches for engineering) plus ad hoc needs (client visits, product launch celebrations, onboarding days). She's building a short list of trusted vendors she rotates between — and she's actively looking for a reliable replacement after her last caterer delivered the wrong order to a 120-person all-hands.

### Goals & Motivations

- Look like the hero: catering that lands on time, impresses leadership, and generates zero complaints
- Build a reliable vendor list she can trust without babysitting every order
- Simplify the ordering process — the fewer emails and calls to confirm, the better
- Accommodate her increasingly diverse workforce: vegan, halal-adjacent, nut allergies, gluten-free
- Stay within budget without getting surprised by hidden delivery or service fees
- Get recurring orders on autopilot so she can focus on higher-priority work

### Pain Points & Frustrations

- Previous caterers delivered generic menus — pizza and sandwiches again — and teams stopped being excited about catered meals
- Dietary labeling failures have caused real incidents: a team member with a nut allergy received unlabeled food
- Last-minute scrambles when preferred vendors cancel or go unavailable
- Hidden fees on invoices — service charges, delivery surcharges — blow her budget and require awkward expense reapprovals
- Coordinating across multiple vendors for a single event (food + drinks) wastes time
- No visibility into delivery status — she can't tell leadership when food will arrive

### What She's Searching For Online

| Channel | Details |
|---|---|
| **Google Searches** | "corporate catering Mountain View" · "office lunch catering San Jose" · "catering for 150 people Bay Area" |
| **Discovery Channels** | LinkedIn vendor recommendations in Office Managers groups · Google Reviews · Referrals from peers in similar roles |
| **Decision Triggers** | A peer recommends a vendor · A previous vendor fails · Company headcount crosses 100+ |
| **Vetting Process** | Checks Google Reviews, looks at menu variety, asks for a sample quote, evaluates response speed |

### What CBC Offers Sarah

- ✓ 5 specialized chef cuisines in one order — no more generic menus, team excitement is back
- ✓ Every item individually labeled (vegan, GF, nut-free) — dietary incident risk eliminated
- ✓ On-time delivery guarantee — if food isn't staged and ready for your team, next order is 10% off
- ✓ Transparent pricing — no hidden fees, no invoice surprises
- ✓ Recurring account setup — weekly order locked in, fixed pricing, zero admin every week
- ✓ Single point of contact — one phone call, one text, one human who knows her account

### Objections & How to Handle Them

**"We already have a caterer"**
> Ask: 'Are you happy with their menu variety and dietary labeling?' Most aren't. Offer a free tasting as a no-risk comparison.

**"Your price is higher than our current vendor"**
> Reframe: 'Our price includes delivery, setup, and full dietary labeling — what's included in their quote?' Almost always uncovers hidden fees on their end.

**"I need to get approval"**
> Provide a one-pager and a sample quote formatted for easy internal forwarding. Offer to jump on a 10-min call with their manager.

**"We're not ready to commit to a contract"**
> Offer a single-event trial with the 10% first-order discount. No commitment required.

### Best Outreach Approach

**Channel Priority: LinkedIn DM → Email → Phone**

Lead with pain, not product. Sarah doesn't want to hear about your chef roster — she wants to know you'll solve her three core problems: reliability, dietary accuracy, and zero hidden fees. The hook that converts:

> *"Your team deserves more than just pizza — and you deserve a caterer who makes your job easier, not harder."*

- **Email 1:** Lead with reliability + dietary labeling promise — her two biggest fears
- **Follow-up:** Include a real menu (Sichuan dim sum buffet, sushi bento boxes) to spark team excitement
- **Offer:** Free chef tasting — low-risk, high-impact way to get her physically engaged with the product
- **Close:** Recurring account proposal — frame it as 'one less thing to manage every week'

### A Day in Marcus's Life

Marcus owns employee experience from end to end: hiring, retention, culture, wellness, and benefits. In 2025, food is a retention strategy. He knows the data — companies with strong food programs see measurably lower attrition, and his CEO is asking him to justify the perks budget before renewals in Q3.

Catering is a line item in Marcus's People Ops budget, not an administrative task. He's not ordering lunch — he's building culture. He evaluates vendors on three things: does the food represent the diversity of our team, does it support wellness goals, and can this vendor scale with us as we grow from 200 to 400 people?

Marcus is also the internal champion who will sell this vendor to finance. He needs numbers — cost per head, projected quarterly spend, ROI narrative — not just a menu PDF.

### Goals & Motivations

- Improve employee retention and satisfaction scores — food is a measurable lever
- Demonstrate ROI on the perks budget to CFO and CEO before Q3 budget review
- Build a recurring catering program that runs on autopilot — not a manual weekly task
- Represent the cultural diversity of a tech workforce (20+ nationalities at his company)
- Align food program with ESG / sustainability commitments the company has made publicly
- Reduce the administrative burden on his team — fewer vendors, simpler processes

### Pain Points & Frustrations

- Generic catering options (pizza, sandwiches) generate low team satisfaction scores — employees comment on this in internal surveys
- Current vendors can't scale: great at 80 people, fall apart at 200+
- No sustainability story — current vendor uses styrofoam packaging, which conflicts with the company's published ESG targets
- Dietary options are an afterthought — a third of his team is vegan, vegetarian, or has documented allergies, and the current setup requires separate ordering
- Multiple vendors for different needs (lunch vs. client events vs. offsites) creates admin complexity and inconsistent quality
- Can't get reliable per-head cost data for budget forecasting — invoices vary too much

| **Vetting Process** | Requests RFP or formal proposal · Needs pricing model for quarterly budget planning · May involve procurement team for contracts over $10K/quarter |

### What CBC Offers Marcus

### Objections & How to Handle Them

**"We need to go through procurement"**
> Provide a formal proposal document with per-head pricing, quarterly projections, and vendor credentials. Offer a reference call with an existing client.

**"Our current vendor is under contract"**
> Ask when the contract renews. Schedule a tasting 60 days before renewal. Stay warm with monthly content touches (menu drops, LinkedIn).

**"We need more than 300 guests for some events"**
> CBC's 300-person max covers 90%+ of events. For overflow, propose a mix-and-match approach across multiple service windows.

**"Can you handle weekly volume reliably?"**
> Lead with the on-time delivery guarantee and the HubSpot order tracking system. Offer a 4-week pilot with no long-term commitment to prove reliability.

**"I need to justify this to the CFO"**
> Provide the ROI framing: average tech catering spend of $18/person vs. cost of one bad hire ($15K+). Food scores on eNPS surveys are measurable retention data.

### Best Outreach Approach

**Channel Priority: LinkedIn Content → LinkedIn DM → Formal Email Proposal**

Marcus is a thought leader consumer. He reads LinkedIn articles, follows HR influencers, and respects vendors who demonstrate they understand the People Ops function — not just catering. The hook that converts:

> *"Your catering vendor is a culture statement. Does yours reflect the diversity of your team?"*

- **LinkedIn:** Publish an article — 'How Corporate Catering Affects Employee Retention Scores' — Marcus will engage with this content before a DM ever lands

- **Offer:** Free tasting for his leadership team — frame it as a team culture experience, not a sales demo
- **Proposal:** Create a formal catering program proposal with quarterly cost projections and a 4-week pilot structure

### How to Identify Which Avatar You're Talking To

Ask one question on your first call: *"Is catering something you manage personally, or does it tie into a broader employee experience program?"*

- If they say **"I manage it personally"** → you're talking to Sarah. Lead with reliability, speed, and simplicity.
- If they mention **"culture," "retention," "perks budget," or "employee experience"** → you're talking to Marcus. Lead with diversity, sustainability, and program design.
- If they mention **"procurement" or "contract approval"** → almost always Marcus. Prepare a formal proposal.
