What Is CBC

Your food.
A new market.

Culinary Block Collective is a corporate catering operation built on top of the Culinary Block commissary. I'm using the kitchen infrastructure we already share to sell your food to Bay Area tech companies.

The companies I'm targeting are 50–300 person tech firms in the South Bay and Peninsula. Office managers and HR teams who need reliable, high-quality catering for team lunches, offsites, and recurring weekly orders.

This is a market that has money and orders consistently. They just need a trusted vendor who shows up on time with great food. That's where you come in.

The opportunity

Tech companies routinely spend $20–$30 per box on corporate catering. They order weekly. They order in volume. And they rarely switch vendors once they find one they trust.

Most of them have never had access to food at the quality level you cook. That gap is the opportunity.

Common Questions

Questions you probably have.

"Why 25%? That seems high."
Sales, operations, branding, coordination, and logistics aren't free. Most distributors and brokers take 30–40%. I'm taking 25% and doing substantially more than a distributor — I'm building the client relationships, handling every client touchpoint, and guaranteeing you get paid. You don't have to do any of that.
"Do I have to take every order you send me?"
No. You tell me your availability. I route orders accordingly. If you can't take a particular order, I find another chef or decline it. There's no minimum commitment and no penalty for passing. The goal is a long-term working relationship — that only works if the arrangement actually fits your schedule.
"What if an order gets cancelled?"
I'm working on a formal cancellation policy and will share the draft before we sign anything. The goal is to protect your prep time — if a client cancels inside a certain window, there should be a fee that covers your cost of prep already done. We'll define the specifics together.
"What if the client has special dietary requirements?"
I handle that conversation with the client. If their requirements are outside what you offer, we decline the order or route it to another chef. You never get a surprise requirement day-of — every dietary spec is confirmed with you before the order is accepted.
"What about my existing customers and business?"
Nothing changes. CBC orders are additive — new clients, new orders, completely separate from whatever you're doing now. You're a contractor, not exclusive to CBC. Your existing business stays yours.
"What commitment am I making if I say yes?"
A simple conversation first — no contract yet. If it makes sense, we sign a contractor agreement that covers the basics: your tier(s), your capacity limits, payment terms, and the delivery protocol. You can take or pass on individual orders based on your schedule. It's a working partnership, not a lock-in.
