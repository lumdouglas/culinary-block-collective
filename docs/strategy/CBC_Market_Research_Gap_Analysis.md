## 2. Key Alignments

- **Pricing sweet spot validated.** The $25–$40 per-person band is confirmed as fastest-growing and underserved. CBC's positioning clears the $22–$24 and $32–$38 "dead zones" cited in the research via chef specialization and labeling differentiators.
- **Cuisine roster is a near-perfect fit.** Research ranks the top 4 in-demand cuisines as Mexican/Cali-Mex, Mediterranean/bowls, Japanese/sushi, and Chinese/Thai/SE Asian. CBC's current roster (Chef John = Mexican Fusion + Italian, Unique Cuisine = Sushi/Japanese, Kivi = Chinese) covers 3 of the top 4 directly.
- **Differentiators address the top buyer complaints.** Late deliveries, poor labeling, repetition, and multi-vendor hassle are the most-cited pain points. CBC's on-time guarantee, individual dietary/FDA labeling, and single-contact multi-chef model are direct solutions — not incremental improvements.
- **AB 660 is a genuine regulatory tailwind.** The law takes effect July 1, 2026. CBC's existing consume-by labeling protocol already meets the standard. Most smaller operators and platform vendors will scramble to comply. This is a sellable advantage from day one.
- **Target buyer personas (Sarah + Marcus) are validated.** Research confirms Office Managers and People Ops are the primary decision-makers, with reliability and dietary transparency as their top priorities — exactly what the Sarah and Marcus personas are built around.
- **Attended service (Tier 2) is a high-margin opportunity.** The +$8/person add-on with ~$30/hr labor cost yields strong ROI on 100+ person events. Research confirms buyer demand for "experiential" catering aligns with this tier.
- **Anchor Day concentration (Tue–Thu) confirmed.** 80%+ of orders fall on Tuesday through Thursday. This validates a focused outreach and fulfillment strategy rather than trying to fill all 5 weekdays.
- **Referral-driven CAC is cheapest.** Research estimates referral CAC at <$200–500 vs. $800–2K for cold outreach. This validates prioritizing post-delivery referral asks and Slack/office manager network penetration alongside cold outbound.

## 3. Gaps, Risks & Refinements

### HIGH PRIORITY

- **Blended commission is ~17.5%, not the 25% the research benchmarks as "favorable."** The research's unit economics section assumes 25% commission + commissary efficiency. CBC's actual weighted commission across the three chefs (12% / 20% / 25%) is materially lower. The $150 service fee helps, but a financial model showing blended margin per order tier is needed before launch to confirm viability. **Action: Build a per-order P&L model at 50, 75, and 100 guests for each chef/tier combo.**

- **Break-even at 9 orders is optimistic without a validated financial model.** Research flags this as aggressive and notes 20–25 orders as a more realistic stability threshold. At 9 orders/month with blended margins, CBC needs to understand: What's the minimum viable AOV? What's the fixed cost base? What happens if Chef John (lowest commission) captures 60%+ of early orders? **Action: Stress-test break-even under pessimistic chef mix and AOV scenarios.**

- **Chef agreements are unsigned — this is the single biggest launch blocker.** Kivi has no agreement. Chef John's draft needs attorney review. Unique Cuisine's negotiated exception needs formal documentation. No agreements = no enforceable non-compete = no protection of client relationships. **Action: Finalize all three agreements before any client-facing activity.**

- **Santa Clara Catering Permit not mentioned in any project file.** Research identifies this as a regulatory requirement (~$282+ fee, requires commissary agreement + SOPs + inspection). This is a potential legal blocker that appears to have been overlooked entirely. **Action: Confirm permit status and begin application immediately.**

- **50-guest minimum may be too high for market entry.** The research shows national avg headcount of ~25 and notes that even in South Bay, many tech companies order for partial teams (30–40 people). A rigid 50-guest minimum cuts out potential entry-point orders, especially for the 75–125 employee segment. **Action: Consider a 30-guest minimum for Kivi (lower price point, higher commission absorbs smaller orders) while holding 50 for premium chefs.**

### MEDIUM PRIORITY

- **No Mediterranean/health bowl option in the roster.** This is the #2 cuisine demand signal in the research (and #1 for wellness/customizable). CBC currently has zero coverage here. This is a rotation gap that could matter for recurring accounts wanting weekly variety. **Action: Scout Mediterranean chef prospects from commissary tenants for Month 2–3 addition.**

- **Cold outbound is the primary sales motion, but referrals have 4x better CAC.** The sales plan (25 outreach messages/week, LinkedIn sequences, cold call scripts) is heavily weighted toward outbound. Research shows referrals via Slack and office manager networks are the dominant discovery channel (~65%) with CAC under $500. The sales plan should weight referral generation at least equally. **Action: Add a structured referral program to the sales playbook — not just "ask 1 happy client for a referral" but a systematic incentive and network-seeding plan.**

- **The $150 service fee is not market-validated.** No competitor pricing data in the research mentions a comparable line-item fee. While it's positioned as transparent (good), a flat $150 on a $1,250 minimum order is 12% of the total — which could cause sticker shock for smaller orders. **Action: Monitor win/loss data on early quotes to see if the fee is a conversion barrier. Consider absorbing it into per-person pricing as a fallback.**

- **Anchor Day strategy is not reflected in current sales ops.** Research strongly recommends concentrating on Tue–Thu, but the sales files, outreach sequences, and HubSpot pipeline don't reference day-of-week targeting or Anchor Day messaging. **Action: Build Anchor Day language into outreach templates and quote follow-ups ("Lock in your Tuesday spread for the quarter").**

- **Churn risk at 20–30% is real and not addressed in ops.** Research cites buyer turnover and quality lapses as primary churn drivers. The operations file has strong food safety and on-time protocols, but no client retention playbook — no NPS tracking, no quarterly check-ins for recurring accounts, no escalation path for quality complaints beyond logging in HubSpot. **Action: Build a retention SOP before Month 3.**

### LOW PRIORITY

- **Research suggests biotech/life sciences as a secondary vertical.** This is mentioned but not reflected in any CBC targeting, persona work, or outreach templates. Not urgent for launch, but worth noting for Month 3+ pipeline diversification.

- **Korean BBQ / poke / ramen bowls flagged as rising fusion demand.** Another roster gap, but lower priority than Mediterranean. Worth tracking as the chef roster grows.

- **Research notes "platform fatigue" as a competitor weakness.** CBC's messaging doesn't explicitly attack this pain point yet. There's an opportunity to add language like "No algorithm. No marketplace. One point of contact" to outbound copy.

## 4. Updated Strategic Takeaways for Launch

### 1. Validate Unit Economics Before Spending on Outreach
The blended commission (~17.5%) is materially lower than the 25% the research assumes for healthy margins. Before investing time in outbound sequences and LinkedIn prospecting, build a per-order P&L model for every chef/tier/guest-count combination. If the math doesn't work for Chef John orders under 75 guests (where CBC nets ~$4/person + $150), you need to know that now — not after your first 10 orders are all Five Course Provision at 50 guests.

### 2. Lead With Labeling + AB 660 as a Regulatory Wedge
AB 660 takes effect July 1, 2026. Most competitors will not be compliant on day one. CBC already is. This is not a nice-to-have differentiator — it's a compliance advantage that de-risks the buyer's decision. Every outbound email, every quote, every tasting follow-up should include a line about AB 660 readiness. Position CBC as the safe, compliant choice: "We're AB 660-compliant before the deadline — is your current vendor?"

### 3. Concentrate on Tue–Thu Anchor Days for 100–200 Employee Firms
The research narrows the bullseye to 100–200 employee tech companies ordering for Anchor Days (Tue–Thu). These firms have reliable budgets ($20K–$75K annual), clear decision-makers (Sarah/Marcus personas), and order at 50–100 guest volumes that hit CBC's sweet spot. Build your first 20 prospects from this exact profile using LinkedIn Sales Navigator filters: Bay Area tech, 100–200 headcount, Office Manager or People Ops title.

### 4. Build Referral Mechanics Into the Sales Engine From Day One
Cold outbound CAC is 4x higher than referrals. The current sales plan treats referrals as an afterthought ("ask 1 happy client for a referral"). Instead: after every successful delivery, execute a structured referral ask — specific language, specific incentive (credit toward next order), specific target ("Do you know an Office Manager at another company who deals with the same catering headaches?"). Seed the Slack/office manager network early; this is how 65% of buyers discover caterers.

### 5. Secure Permits and Agreements Before Any Client Touches You
Three items must close before the first dollar of outreach effort: (a) all three chef agreements signed with non-compete clauses, (b) Santa Clara Catering Permit application filed and progressing, (c) attorney review of Chef John's draft agreement completed. If any chef walks with a CBC-introduced client before the non-compete is signed, the entire commission model breaks. If CBC is operating without the proper permit, one complaint shuts you down. These are not "nice to have before launch" — they are legal prerequisites.
