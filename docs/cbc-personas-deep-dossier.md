# CBC Buyer Personas — Deep Psychological Dossier

> **Purpose:** A targeting-grade reference document. Written so that if Sarah or Marcus read their own section, they'd think *"who's been watching me?"* Use this to write subject lines, frame objections, choose proof points, and decide what to leave out. Companion to `personas.md` (the structured version), not a replacement.

---

## How to use this file

Most persona docs are written *about* the buyer. This one is written largely *as* the buyer — first person, internal monologue, the things they wouldn't say in a sales call but would say to a peer over a beer. When you draft an email, a landing page, or a follow-up, open this file, find the relevant section, and ask: *"Would Sarah/Marcus nod at this, or would they roll their eyes?"* If you can't tell, you're not ready to send it.

Three rules:

1. **Never sell to the title. Sell to the fear and the calendar.** Sarah's title is Office Manager. Her actual job is "absorb chaos so the executive team doesn't see it." Marcus's title is HR Director. His actual job is "defend the People budget in front of a CFO who thinks perks are waste." Speak to the actual job.
2. **Match the clock speed.** Sarah responds in minutes when she's panicked and not at all when she's not. Marcus responds in weeks but remembers everything. Different cadences, different content, different CTAs.
3. **Specificity is the only thing they trust.** Both buyers have been burned. Generic vendor language ("reliable," "fresh," "curated") is invisible to them. Numbers, named guarantees, and concrete operational details are what break through.

# AVATAR 1 · Sarah Nguyen — Office Manager / EA

## The 60-second snapshot

Sarah is the person at a 120-person Series B startup who knows where the spare HDMI cables are, which conference room has the wonky thermostat, and what the CEO's wife is allergic to. She has no direct reports and she runs the company's physical and operational reality. Catering is maybe 8% of her job and 40% of her visibility — when it goes well, no one notices; when it goes wrong, everyone notices and she's the one who has to explain it in Slack. She is not looking for a "catering partner." She is looking for one fewer thing that can blow up on her on a Tuesday.

## A day in Sarah's life — first person

> *6:42 AM.* My alarm goes off and the first thing I do — before I'm even awake — is check Slack on my phone. Not because I'm supposed to. Because if there's a fire I need a head start. This morning: nothing in #office, two DMs from engineers about parking, and a message from our CFO in #ops that just says "can we chat today?" with no context. Great. Now I get to spend my shower spiraling about whether it's about the Q3 office budget or whether I screwed something up.
>
> *7:30 AM.* On the train. I open my "morning sweep" — Gmail, Slack, the shared #catering-vendors channel I built so people stop DMing me individual lunch requests, and the Google Sheet where I track every recurring order for the next 6 weeks. The sheet is color-coded. Red means I haven't confirmed yet. There are three reds for next week and I feel that little catch in my chest.
>
> *8:15 AM.* At my desk. I'm the second person in. I make the coffee — yes, literally, because the office coffee program is also mine — and I do my walkthrough. Conference rooms reset, whiteboards wiped, snack stations restocked, the broken chair in the phone booth that I've been trying to get replaced for six weeks is still broken. I take a photo and text the facilities vendor again. They'll respond in three days.
>
> *8:45 AM.* My first calendar event: "Catering for Thursday all-hands — confirm." This is the one I've been dreading. We have 138 people coming. Last month our caterer sent food for 100 and I had to send an intern to Whole Foods with a company card. The CEO made a joke about it in front of the whole company. He was smiling but I was not. I have not used that vendor since. I'm trying a new one Thursday and I am 70% sure it will be fine and 30% sure I am going to have a panic attack at 11:47 AM when the food isn't there yet.
>
> *9:00–11:00 AM.* Slack is on fire. The wifi in the east conference room is down (not my job but somehow my job). A new hire's laptop didn't arrive (not my job but somehow my job). Our COO needs me to find a venue for an offsite for 40 people in three weeks under $8K including AV (definitely my job and I will spend the next four hours of my life on this). Someone DMs me to ask if we can do "something fun" for Thursday's lunch because morale is low. Define fun. With what budget. With what notice.
>
> *11:30 AM.* I finally get to my email. 47 unread. Six are from caterers I've never heard of pitching me. I delete five of them in under two seconds — they all say the same thing, "premium catering for your team," generic photos of grain bowls, no pricing, no specifics. The sixth one has a subject line that mentions a real problem I have so I open it. I don't reply but I screenshot it and put it in a folder called "maybe later."
>
> *12:15 PM.* Today's catered lunch arrives. I am the one who meets the driver. I am the one who checks the order against the invoice. I am the one who sets up the chafing trays, organizes the labels, makes sure the vegan options are visible and not hidden behind the chicken. No one helps. No one offers. When it's done and people are eating I take a photo for our internal #culture channel and tag it #lunchwin and that's the entire credit I get for two hours of work.
>
> *1:30 PM.* The CEO walks by and says "this is great, who's the vendor?" I tell him. He says "nice, let's use them more." That sentence just added an item to my Q4 to-do list with no further instructions. I have to figure out what "more" means and build a recurring program around it without him ever thinking about it again. If I do this well he will never mention it. If I do it badly I will hear about it in my next 1:1.
>
> *2:00–4:30 PM.* Vendor follow-ups, expense reports, a furniture delivery, a fire drill (literal — building-wide), and someone asks me to order a cake for a birthday tomorrow that I did not know about. I order the cake. I do not get thanked.
>
> *5:15 PM.* I do my "tomorrow prep" — confirm tomorrow's deliveries, lay out badges for two visitors, send the catering headcount update for Thursday. I notice the new caterer hasn't replied to my confirmation request from this morning. I send a follow-up. Politely. Inside, I am thinking *please please please don't be flaky, I cannot do another all-hands disaster, I will literally cry.*
>
> *6:30 PM.* I'm home. My partner asks how my day was. I say "fine" because explaining it would take longer than I have energy for. I check Slack one more time before bed. The new caterer replied. They confirmed. I exhale. I'll worry again at 7 AM tomorrow.

## What's actually going on inside Sarah's head

**Her job is invisible labor and she knows it.** Every win is silent. Every loss is loud. This shapes every vendor decision: she is buying *risk reduction*, not food. The caterer who eliminates the chance of a public failure is worth a 15% premium over the one who's just cheaper. She won't say this out loud — she'll say "we need to stay in budget" — but if you give her real airtight reliability she will find the budget.

**She has been burned and her threat-detection is on hair-trigger.** When she reads your cold email, the part of her brain evaluating you is asking one question: *is this person going to be one more thing I have to manage, or one less?* Every claim you make that sounds like every other vendor's claim is filed under "more work, probably a liar." She doesn't need to be sold. She needs to be *de-risked*.

**She is terrified of looking bad in front of leadership.** Not in a deep psychological way — in a practical, my-promotion-track way. Office Managers at Series B startups have a path: OM → Head of Workplace → Director of Workplace Experience → maybe COS or VP Ops. Every public failure is a notch against that path. Every smooth quarter is a notch toward it. Catering is not abstract to her; it's career equity.

**Her boss doesn't understand what she does.** Her VP Ops or COO has never planned a 150-person lunch. They think it's "ordering food." When something goes wrong they ask "how did this happen?" in a tone that implies it should have been impossible. Sarah carries that gap silently. Anyone who *gets* the operational complexity of her job — without her having to explain it — earns instant trust.

**She is not a foodie.** She doesn't care about the chef's training or the provenance of the protein. She cares whether the team will be excited, whether the dietary labels are right, and whether the trays will look full at 12:01 PM. Don't waste her time with culinary storytelling on the first touch. Save it for the tasting.

**She is, however, a quiet aesthete.** The reason she takes that #lunchwin photo is because it's her one form of authorship in a job otherwise made of invisible plumbing. A vendor whose food *photographs well* — colorful, varied, abundant-looking — is giving her a small gift she won't articulate but will repay in loyalty.

**She is desperately under-tooled.** Most OMs run their entire job out of Gmail, Slack, a Google Sheet, and notebook scribbles. She would love a dashboard. She would love a portal. She would love an account page where she can see her next four orders at a glance. But she has been pitched "platforms" before that turned out to be more work than email. Promise her simplicity, deliver simplicity, and she will not leave you for years.

## Her incentives — what she's actually optimizing for

| Incentive | Surface explanation | What it actually means |
|---|---|---|
| Stay in budget | "We have to hit our quarterly numbers" | Avoid expense reports that get flagged and force her to email finance to explain |
| No complaints | "We want the team to be happy" | No one slacks her negatively, no one mentions food in the next eNPS survey |
| Look competent | "I just want it to go smoothly" | The CEO and COO never think about catering at all, which is the highest praise she will ever get |
| Predictability | "We need recurring options" | She wants to delete an entire recurring task from her cognitive load |
| Speed of response | "Quick turnaround is important" | When she texts at 8 AM about an emergency, she needs an answer by 8:15, not 11 |

## Sarah's information environment

- **What she opens:** emails with subject lines that name a specific pain (*"For Office Managers who got burned by their last caterer"*), referrals from peers in OM Slack groups, anything from a vendor a colleague has personally vouched for.
- **What she deletes in two seconds:** anything with "premium," "curated," "elevated," or "white-glove" in the subject. Anything with stock photos of grain bowls. Anything addressed to "Hi there." Anything longer than a phone screen.
- **What she screenshots and saves:** specific guarantees (the 10% off late guarantee is exactly the kind of thing she screenshots), one-pagers with clear pricing, anything that makes her think "I might need this in three months."
- **Where she actually finds vendors:** the #office-managers Slack community, peer DMs ("hey who do you use for catering?"), Google reviews (she really does read them, and she filters out anything under 4.6 stars), and LinkedIn posts from other OMs talking about vendor experiences. She does NOT find vendors through display ads, podcasts, or thought leadership content.
- **Her phone habits:** she checks Slack and email constantly during the day but rarely responds to non-urgent things until the 11:30 AM email window or after 5 PM. A cold email sent at 8 AM Tuesday will be seen at 11:30 AM Tuesday or never.

## The unspoken stuff

- She is probably a woman, probably between 28 and 38, probably the only OM at her company, and probably feels like the most professionally lonely person in the building. There is no one else in her function to compare notes with. This is why OM Slack groups and LinkedIn communities matter so much — they are her peer group.
- She is paid less than she should be and she knows it. She sees the engineering salaries and the sales OTEs and she knows her work is what makes the office function and she also knows it will never be valued the same way. This makes her *extra* sensitive to vendors who treat her with respect vs. vendors who talk down to her or assume she's not the decision-maker.
- She has been mistaken for a receptionist, an assistant, a "girl who orders the food," and worse. When you address her as a professional operations leader — because that's what she is — you're already ahead of 90% of your competitors.
- She is going to test you with one small order before she trusts you with anything important. If you fumble that small order, you are dead to her, forever, with no appeal. If you nail it, she will start telling other OMs about you within two weeks.

## How Sarah actually makes the buying decision

1. **Trigger event** — usually a vendor failure, sometimes a headcount jump, occasionally a CEO comment.
2. **3–5 vendors get on her radar** through Google, peer recs, or a cold email that happened to land in her 11:30 window on the right day.
3. **Vetting takes 20 minutes total** — she clicks the website, looks at menu photos, scans reviews, and forms a gut judgment. She is NOT comparing spreadsheets. Most vendors lose at this stage because their site looks generic or their menu photos look like every other caterer.
4. **She requests a quote from 2–3** and the one that responds *fastest with specifics* (not "I'll get back to you tomorrow with details") wins the next step.
5. **She books a small order as a test** — a team lunch, not the all-hands. This is the audition. She is watching for: arrival time vs. promised time, accuracy of the order, dietary labeling quality, whether the labels are individually on items or just a printed sheet, how the trays look on her setup, and whether the driver was friendly to her in the lobby.
6. **If the test goes well, she expands within 30 days** — usually starting with a recurring weekly order, then layering in larger events. If it goes badly, she ghosts.
7. **At 90 days she'll start referring you to other OMs**, unprompted, if you've stayed perfect. This is your real growth channel.

## Drafting language that lands with Sarah

**Subject lines that work:**
- "For OMs who got burned by their last 100+ person caterer"
- "48hr notice catering for Bay Area offices — actually, not just on the website"
- "Quick question about your Thursday lunch program"
- "Saw you're hiring at [Company] — quick catering note"

**Subject lines that fail:**
- "Premium curated catering for your team"
- "Elevate your office lunch experience"
- "Introducing CBC"
- Anything with an emoji in the subject line

**Opening lines that land:**
- "I know you've probably had a vendor promise reliability and then fail you on a 100+ person order. I want to tell you exactly how we make sure that doesn't happen — in two paragraphs, not a sales pitch."
- "I'll be brief because I know your inbox is brutal."

**CTAs that convert:**
- "Want me to send a sample quote for your next all-hands? No call required, I just need headcount and date."
- "Free 25-person tasting at your office, on us, no strings — yes or no?"

**CTAs that flop:**
- "Let's schedule a 30-minute discovery call"
- "Book time on my calendar"
- "Let's find a time to chat about your needs"

# AVATAR 2 · Marcus Chen — Head of People / HR Director

## The 60-second snapshot

Marcus runs People Ops at a 220-person mid-stage tech company. He owns hiring, retention, culture, comp philosophy, performance management, perks, and increasingly, the politically loaded job of justifying every dollar of "employee experience" spend to a CFO who thinks the whole function is overhead. Catering, to him, is not lunch. It's a *lever*. It shows up in eNPS data, it shows up in Glassdoor reviews, it shows up in the stories candidates tell when they accept or decline offers. He thinks in programs, not orders. He buys slowly, deliberately, and expects to be treated as a strategic partner, not a transaction.

## A day in Marcus's life — first person

> *6:15 AM.* I'm up before my kids. I make coffee and spend 25 minutes reading — Lattice's blog, the latest People Ops newsletter, a Substack from a Head of People I respect, and then a quick scan of LinkedIn. I'm not really reading for fun. I'm reading for ammunition. Every quarter I have to walk into a meeting with my CEO and CFO and defend why we should keep investing in people programs when revenue growth is slowing, and the only thing that wins those meetings is data and frameworks I can borrow from someone smarter.
>
> *7:30 AM.* I drop the kids at school and get to the office by 8:30. On the drive I listen to a podcast — usually something with a CHRO from a bigger company. I'm not trying to copy them; I'm trying to figure out what the next stage of my career looks like. I'm 41. I was a generalist HRBP for years and I made the jump to Head of People here 18 months ago. This is the role where I either prove I can be a VP People at a 500-person company or I get stuck.
>
> *8:30 AM.* First thing in the office: I check our ATS for the overnight applications, scan the engineering hiring pipeline, and look at our people dashboard. Headcount is at 218. We had two regrettable departures last month — one from engineering, one from product. Both cited "culture" in their exits. The word "culture" is a knife to me. It's vague, it's unfalsifiable, and it's how my function gets blamed for things that aren't actually about my function.
>
> *9:00 AM.* Stand-up with my team — three HRBPs, a recruiter, and a People Ops coordinator. We talk through the week. Q3 perks budget is up for review. Our current catering vendor has been mediocre — they're consistent but the food is boring and our last all-hands had three people complain in the post-event survey about lack of vegetarian variety. I've been meaning to look into alternatives for a month and keep getting pulled into other fires.
>
> *10:00 AM.* I have a 1:1 with our CEO. He brings up retention — we're at 88% annualized and he wants 92%+. He says "what are we actually doing differently this quarter?" and I have to have an answer. I walk him through our re-engineered onboarding, the new manager training, the planned wellness pilot. He nods. Then he says "and what about food, because I keep hearing about it?" — and I realize the food problem has now reached the CEO's ear. That changes my week.
>
> *11:00 AM.* I open my catering vendor research doc. It has nine names in it. I've been collecting them passively for months — some from LinkedIn, some from peer recommendations in my Heads of People Slack group, two from cold emails I saved because they actually said something interesting. I rank them in my head. I'm looking for: cultural variety in cuisines, sustainability story I can put in our quarterly ESG note, real per-head pricing (not "starting at"), and proof they can scale through 300 people, because we'll be there by next Q2.
>
> *12:00 PM.* I eat at my desk. I'm reviewing two offer letters, checking on a sensitive employee relations situation, and drafting talking points for the all-hands tomorrow. The catering doc is open in another tab. I don't have time to deal with it today.
>
> *1:30 PM.* Calibration meeting with engineering managers about performance reviews. Two hours of judgment calls about people's careers. I leave drained. This is the part of my job nobody talks about — the moral weight of being in the room when decisions are made about someone's livelihood.
>
> *3:30 PM.* Back at my desk. Slack is full. Three managers need advice. Our recruiting lead has a candidate question. A finance partner DMs me asking for the headcount plan reconciliation for Q4 — by Friday. I add it to my list. I am tired and we are not even at the hardest part of the day.
>
> *4:00 PM.* I open LinkedIn and post a short reflection on something I read this morning about psychological safety. I do this 2–3x a week, deliberately. It's not vanity. It's career capital — every recruiter, peer, and CHRO who might hire me into my next role is in my LinkedIn feed, and I want to be visible as a thinker, not just an operator. I get a few likes within the hour. One of them is from a Head of People at a 600-person company I'd love to work for someday.
>
> *4:45 PM.* I notice a new connection request — and a thoughtful DM — from someone at a catering company. They've referenced something specific from our company blog about our sustainability commitments. They're not pitching. They're asking a real question. I appreciate that. I save the DM to respond to tomorrow.
>
> *5:30 PM.* Wrap up. I leave the office by 6 to get home for dinner with my kids. Family time is non-negotiable; it's also one of the things I'm most rigorous about modeling because I tell every employee here to protect their own boundaries and I would be a hypocrite if I didn't.
>
> *9:00 PM.* Kids in bed. I open my laptop one more time. I respond to two Slack DMs from this morning, finish a short doc for tomorrow's all-hands, and then I scroll my LinkedIn feed for ten minutes. I see another People Ops thought leader's post about how catering is the most under-leveraged retention tool. I screenshot it. I'll use that framing in my Q3 budget defense. I close the laptop at 9:45 and feel a low buzz of satisfaction that I've moved the chess pieces forward by half a square today, which on most days is the most you can hope for in this job.

## What's actually going on inside Marcus's head

**He is constantly building a defensible case.** Marcus doesn't make decisions; he makes *cases*. Every vendor, every program, every line item — when he commits to it, he's already drafted in his head the half-page he'll show his CFO if questioned. If you give him the case pre-built (per-head numbers, retention framing, ESG line, comparison data), you save him hours of work and you become his preferred choice not because you're cheapest but because you're *easiest to defend*.

**He is in an identity contest with the CFO.** Behind every Marcus is a CFO who, in calmer moments, agrees that culture matters, but who in the actual budget meeting will say "show me the ROI." Marcus is constantly losing tiny political battles to that voice. He needs vendors who arm him with ammunition. The vendor who hands him a one-page "ROI of food programs" deck *that he can directly forward* is doing free strategic work for him, and he will repay that loyalty.

**He is a thought-leader consumer and a modest thought-leader producer.** He reads the People Ops content economy daily. He posts on LinkedIn 2–4 times a week. He follows specific voices (Lattice, Charthop, Donut, certain CHROs at brand-name companies). The way you reach Marcus is not through cold outreach in isolation — it's through cold outreach that *follows* months of him passively seeing your name in the content stream. By the time the DM lands, he should already half-recognize you.

**He thinks in programs, not orders.** Sarah thinks "Thursday lunch." Marcus thinks "Q3 employee experience program with measurable touchpoints." When you pitch Marcus, you should never lead with a single event. Lead with a quarter. Lead with a year. Lead with a roadmap. He wants to buy a relationship and a framework, not a transaction.

**He is performing diversity competence and he knows authentic diversity work is hard.** Marcus genuinely cares about DEI. He also knows that the wrong vendor — pizza and sandwiches at a company with 22 nationalities — sends a *signal* about whose culture gets centered. He is not going to say "I need a multi-cuisine caterer because of DEI optics." He will say "we have a diverse team and we want food that reflects that." Same thing. Read it correctly.

**He is allergic to vendor sales energy.** He has been pitched by a hundred SaaS reps in the last three years and he can smell a sales sequence from the subject line. Vendors who write to him like a peer — not a buyer — have a 10x advantage. He will respond to an HRBP-to-HRBP message ten times faster than a sales-to-prospect message, even if the content is identical.

**He is anxious about scale.** His company is going from 220 → 350 → 500 over the next 18–24 months if everything goes right. He needs vendors who will scale with him. He has been burned by tools and partners that worked at 100 people and broke at 250. The single biggest unspoken question he asks every vendor is *"will you still be the right fit when I'm twice as big?"* Answer that question — proactively, with specifics — and you remove his deepest objection.

**He is lonely in a different way than Sarah is lonely.** Sarah is lonely because no one else does her job. Marcus is lonely because he's the only person in his company who thinks about People Ops as a strategic discipline; everyone else thinks of HR as compliance and benefits. He has a peer group, but it's outside his company — Slack groups, conference acquaintances, LinkedIn connections. Earn your way into being a *peer voice* in his ear, not a vendor in his inbox.

## His incentives — what he's actually optimizing for

| Incentive | Surface explanation | What it actually means |
|---|---|---|
| Improve retention | "Food is part of the employee experience" | Lower regrettable attrition gives him political capital and saves the company $15K+ per saved hire; he can show this in his quarterly review |
| Defend the budget | "We need ROI on perks spend" | He needs ammunition for the standing fight with the CFO over whether People Ops is overhead or investment |
| Tell a culture story | "Food represents our values" | He needs concrete artifacts (vendor diversity, sustainability cert, dietary inclusion) to put in board decks, ESG reports, and recruiting collateral |
| Reduce vendor sprawl | "We want a single trusted partner" | His coordinator is overworked and every vendor is admin overhead; consolidating saves headcount cost |
| Personal career growth | (never said out loud) | Every visible win — a smooth program, a quotable result, a thought-leadership angle — moves him toward his next role at a bigger company |

## Marcus's information environment

- **What he opens:** anything from a peer or referrer, anything that names a specific company initiative he's publicly committed to (his ESG report, a culture award, a public hiring milestone), anything from a name he already recognizes from LinkedIn, and short DMs that lead with a question rather than a pitch.
- **What he deletes:** "Hi Marcus, I hope this email finds you well." Anything that mentions "synergies." Anything that opens with "I noticed your company is growing." Anything generic enough that he can tell it's a sequence.
- **What he saves:** ROI frameworks, data points he can cite, one-pagers with quarterly cost models, and any vendor that has clearly read his company's actual public materials.
- **Where he actually finds vendors:** People Ops Slack communities (Lattice, RfH, People Geeks), LinkedIn (60% of his vendor discovery happens here, mostly through content engagement before any direct outreach), peer DMs in his HRBP network, and once or twice a year a conference connection. He does NOT find vendors through Google search the way Sarah does — by the time he Googles, he's already pre-decided.
- **His clock speed:** he reads everything but responds to almost nothing in less than 3 days, and he has zero guilt about it. Cold email cadences should be patient — touches every 2 weeks for ~3 months is the right rhythm, not 3 emails in 5 days.

## The unspoken stuff

- He is being measured on retention numbers that he only partially controls. When attrition spikes for reasons that have nothing to do with him (a bad manager, a comp cycle, a market shift), he still wears it. This is why he'll latch onto any *measurable* lever — like a food program with eNPS pre/post data — that gives him a story he can tell.
- He is one bad quarter away from being told to cut his budget by 20%. He is constantly pre-defending against that. Every vendor decision is filtered through "if I get told to cut, can I justify keeping this one?"
- He has an MBA or is thinking about getting one. He reads HBR articles on his commute. He cares about being seen as strategic, not operational. Treat him as a strategist and he opens up; treat him as a procurement contact and he goes cold.
- He is genuinely values-driven about culture and DEI — and he is also professionally cynical about how much of corporate culture work is theater. He has both modes running simultaneously. The vendors who win with him are the ones who satisfy *both* — real substance + real artifacts he can use.
- He probably has a CHRO ambition. Even if he doesn't say it. Every vendor who helps him build the kind of People program that's resume-worthy is investing in his career, and he'll remember it.

## How Marcus actually makes the buying decision

1. **Passive awareness** — months of seeing your name in his LinkedIn feed, in Slack communities, in content his peers share. By the time you reach out, he should already have a vague positive impression.
2. **Trigger event** — usually a CFO conversation, a board prep, a bad eNPS score, or a CEO question about a specific perk. The trigger creates urgency on a project he's been passive about.
3. **He builds a short list of 4–6 vendors** over 2–4 weeks. This list comes from his saved DMs, his vendor research doc, peer recommendations, and one or two cold emails that earned a place by being unusually substantive.
4. **He requests proposals** — formal, written, with quarterly cost projections. He compares 3 of them on a spreadsheet his coordinator builds. This phase takes 2–3 weeks and you will not hear from him during it; do NOT push.
5. **He books a tasting** with the top 1–2 — and uses it as both a vendor evaluation and a small culture event for his leadership team. Make the tasting *feel* like a culture experience, not a sales demo, and you've already won.
6. **He proposes a 4-week or 90-day pilot** to his CFO with a clear success metric (eNPS, satisfaction survey, cost-per-head benchmark). This is the moment to give him the pre-built ROI doc.
7. **If pilot succeeds, he commits to a quarterly or annual program** and brings you in as a "partner," not a vendor — that language matters to him. From here, he becomes a referral engine inside his peer network, but only if you continue to feed him content and frameworks he can use.

- "Question about [Company]'s Q3 People program"
- "Saw your post on retention — small thought"
- "ESG question for a Head of People"
- "Culture-as-perk: a framework I think you'd push back on"

**Subject lines that fail:**
- "Premium catering for your team"
- "Let's schedule a quick call"
- "Free tasting for [Company]"
- "Following up on my last email"

**Opening lines that land:**
- "I read your company's most recent ESG note — the line about supplier diversity stuck with me. I have a question, not a pitch."
- "I'm not going to ask for a meeting in this email. I want to share one framework on food-as-retention that I think is either useful or wrong, and I'd value your read."

**CTAs that convert:**
- "If it's useful, I can send a one-page model for what a quarterly program would cost at your headcount. No call needed."
- "Want me to put together an ROI framework you can use in your next budget conversation, even if you don't end up working with us?"
- "Open to a tasting *as a leadership team experience*, not a sales meeting?"

**CTAs that flop:**
- "Let's hop on a 15-minute call"
- "When are you free this week?"
- "I'd love to learn more about your needs"

# Side-by-side: how to know which one you're talking to

| Signal | Sarah | Marcus |
|---|---|---|
| Title on LinkedIn | Office Manager, EA, Workplace Coordinator, Head of Workplace | Head of People, VP People, HR Director, People Ops Lead |
| Email response time | Minutes if urgent, never if not | 3–10 days, consistently |
| First question they ask | "Can you do Thursday?" | "How do you think about scaling with a growing team?" |
| Type of pain they admit | Specific, recent, concrete failure | Strategic, abstract, framework-shaped |
| What impresses them | A guarantee with money behind it | A framework with data behind it |

# Targeting playbook (operator notes for Doug)

1. **Build two tracks, not one.** Sarah-track and Marcus-track should be entirely different sequences — different subject lines, different cadences, different CTAs, different landing pages if you can swing it. Mixing them is the most common reason B2B catering outreach fails.

2. **Sarah-track is high-volume, low-touch, fast-feedback.** Aim for 30–50 outbound touches a week, expect a 4–8% positive reply rate when the messaging is sharp, and convert replies into a small first order within 14 days. The whole funnel is calibrated for speed.

3. **Marcus-track is low-volume, high-touch, slow-feedback.** Aim for 5–10 *deep* touches a week — researched, personalized, content-anchored. Expect a 1–3% reply rate but a much larger eventual deal. Track each Marcus prospect as a named account in HubSpot with multi-month nurture.

4. **The LinkedIn content layer is shared but targeted.** Posts about *operational reliability and OM-life* serve Sarah's awareness. Posts about *culture, retention, and ESG* serve Marcus's awareness. You should be publishing both, on a 2:1 Marcus:Sarah ratio (Marcus reads more, Sarah scrolls less).

5. **Build two one-pagers.** One for Sarah ("the reliability promise" — guarantees, response time, dietary labeling, 48-hour minimum, single point of contact) and one for Marcus ("the program" — quarterly cost model, ESG line, dietary inclusion, scale ceiling, retention framing). They are different documents. Don't try to make one PDF do both jobs.

6. **The tasting is your conversion event for both — but it's a different tasting.** For Sarah, the tasting is a *low-pressure, fast, no-strings* event — she wants to confirm food quality and walk away. For Marcus, the tasting is a *staged leadership-team experience* you co-design with him as a culture moment. Same food, completely different framing.

7. **Reference the buyer's actual world.** For Sarah, that means specific operational language — "8 AM all-hands panic," "the Whole Foods run," "the labels facing the wrong way on the chafing tray." For Marcus, that means specific strategic language — "eNPS food sub-score," "perks budget defense," "vendor consolidation," "scale ceiling at 300." The two vocabularies should never cross over.

8. **Watch for the dual-buyer scenario.** At 150–250-person companies, sometimes Sarah and Marcus exist together — Sarah handles the order, Marcus owns the budget. When this happens, you sell to *both*, with different angles, and you make sure each one feels like the protagonist of the conversation. The deal closes when Marcus is convinced strategically and Sarah is convinced operationally.
