# OM Identification Field Guide

> **Purpose:** Read a LinkedIn profile, an email reply, or a social feed and classify the OM in under 60 seconds. Then respond in the language that fits *that specific subtype* — not generic "Sarah" copy. Companion to `cbc-personas-deep-dossier.md`.

---

## The core insight

"Sarah" is not one person. She is five overlapping subtypes with very different psychologies, career arcs, and communication styles. Sending the same email to all of them is the same mistake as sending the same email to Sarah and Marcus. The reliability promise lands on every subtype, but **what wraps around the promise** has to change.

There are five OM subtypes you'll encounter in the Bay Area corporate market:

1. **The Veteran Operator** — done this 5+ years, low patience, high standards
2. **The Promoted EA** — calendar-trained, exec-adjacent, still half-thinks like an assistant
3. **The Accidental OM** — new to the role, often the company's first, anxious and open
4. **The Workplace Experience Builder** — treats it as a discipline, brand-conscious, ambitious
5. **The OM/HR Hybrid** — at smaller companies, wears two hats, thinks like a mini-Marcus

Below: how to spot each one, what they actually want, and how to respond.

# Part 1 · The Five OM Subtypes

## Subtype A · The Veteran Operator

### Spot her in 10 seconds

**LinkedIn signals:**
- 5–12+ years of OM/Workplace experience across 2–4 companies
- Job titles progress: Office Coordinator → Office Manager → Sr. Office Manager → Head of Workplace
- Companies on her resume tend to be recognizable mid-stage tech (Stripe-tier, not Fortune 500, not pre-seed)
- Education: often a state school or non-elite, sometimes no college listed at all — she's earned her seat through reps
- Almost no LinkedIn posts of her own; occasional reshares of peer content
- Connections in the 500–1500 range, mostly other OMs and vendors
- Profile photo is professional but not glamour — she's not building a personal brand

**Email signals:**
- Replies are short, directive, no pleasantries
- Uses operational shorthand: "HC 142, 11:30 drop, dock B, NOT loading dock A"
- Will ask for COI (Certificate of Insurance) and W-9 in the first reply if she's interested
- Asks specific operational questions you've never been asked before ("what's your driver no-show protocol?")
- Will name-drop a previous vendor she fired and tell you why

**Social signals:**
- Instagram/Facebook usually private. LinkedIn is the only window.
- May be active in OM Slack groups (Workplace Innovators Network, OM Hangout) — if you can see her there, you can sometimes see her vendor opinions

### What she actually wants

She wants to find one or two anchor vendors and never think about the category again. She has been burned before and developed a sixth sense for which vendors will eventually disappoint her. She is not impressed by sales energy, branding, or "story." She is impressed by *operational maturity* — your COI being current, your driver showing up when promised, your invoice matching your quote. She is buying competence, full stop.

### How she communicates

- Direct, fast, occasionally curt
- Will not soften feedback — if you mess up, you'll hear it in two sentences with no emotional cushioning
- Will not return your call until she has time to give you the right answer; do not interpret silence as disinterest
- Respects vendors who match her tone — don't be warmer than she is, it reads as inauthentic

### How to respond to her

**Subject line:** *"Quick question — your dock B access"* or *"COI + sample quote attached, 90 seconds to read"*

**First message structure:**
1. State your value in one sentence
2. Pre-answer her three biggest operational questions before she asks
3. Attach the COI, the sample quote, the driver protocol doc
4. Offer one CTA: "Want me to put a small test order on the calendar?"
5. Sign off. No closing pleasantries.

**Avoid:**
- "I hope this email finds you well"
- Long backstory about CBC
- Asking her about her "needs"
- Multiple CTAs
- Any mention of "partnership" or "relationship"

**Cadence:** 1 email, 1 follow-up 5 days later, 1 final touch 10 days after that. If no response, drop her into a slow nurture (1 touch every 6 weeks). She'll surface when she's ready.

**Trigger to escalate:** If she replies asking for a COI, you have a 70% chance of winning her with a same-day response. Treat the COI request as a buy signal, not a stall.

## Subtype B · The Promoted EA

### Spot her in 10 seconds

**LinkedIn signals:**
- Career arc: Receptionist → Executive Assistant → EA + Office Coordinator → Office Manager
- Often spent 3–6 years as an EA to a single executive before transitioning
- Current title is sometimes hybrid: "EA to CEO + Office Manager" or "Chief of Staff to CEO"
- Skills section heavy on calendar management, travel, expense reports, event planning
- Recommendations on her profile are from executives, not peers — and they read like glowing personal references, not professional ones
- Posts occasionally about executive support, leadership lessons, "behind the scenes" content
- Profile photo is polished, often professionally taken

**Email signals:**
- Polished, complete sentences, proper salutations
- Often replies on behalf of an executive ("I'm reaching out on behalf of [CEO name] regarding…")
- Will mention the executive's preferences explicitly ("our CEO is gluten-free")
- Response times are extremely fast during business hours, near-zero outside them
- Calendar-link culture: she'll send you a Calendly within two replies

**Social signals:**
- Active on LinkedIn with executive-adjacent content
- Sometimes a personal Instagram with professional polish (work events, team photos, the company culture she's helping build)

### What she actually wants

She wants the catering decision to *make her executive look good*. The food is not for the team, in her mind — it's for the room her CEO will be in. She wants vendors who understand executive presence, who can handle a last-minute VIP visit gracefully, and who will treat her with the same respect she gives her executive. She is also quietly building the case to her boss that she should be promoted from EA to a full Workplace role, and a successful catering program is one of her resume items.

### How she communicates

- Warm, deferential to authority, but firm about her boss's preferences
- Heavy use of "we" — "we want," "we're looking for" — where "we" usually means her and her exec
- Will copy her executive on emails strategically (sometimes as a power move, sometimes as a CYA)
- Cares about the *aesthetics* of the interaction — your email signature, your calendar link, your branded one-pager all matter to her in a way they don't to the Veteran

### How to respond to her

**Subject line:** *"For [Company]'s leadership lunches — a 2-min note"* or *"Catering options for your CEO's Thursday meeting"*

**First message structure:**
1. Acknowledge her role with respect — "I know you're the gatekeeper for a lot more than catering"
2. Lead with VIP / executive-presence positioning
3. Mention attended service and the chef-on-site option
4. Offer to send a printed one-pager she can hand to her exec
5. CTA: a tasting framed as "a chance for your leadership team to experience the food before any commitment"

**Avoid:**
- Treating her like a secretary or gatekeeper to be "gotten past"
- Excessive operational detail — she'll forward you to facilities if she wants that
- Pressuring her on timeline — she runs on her exec's calendar, not yours
- Generic "ROI" framing — that's Marcus's language, not hers

**Cadence:** Weekly touches for 3 weeks, then monthly. She is high-courtesy and will respond eventually if you stay polite and don't burn the goodwill.

**Trigger to escalate:** When she mentions her executive by name or asks if you can accommodate a specific exec preference (dietary, scheduling), you're in. Send a personalized response within an hour.

## Subtype C · The Accidental OM

### Spot her in 10 seconds

**LinkedIn signals:**
- 0–18 months in the OM role
- Previous role was something *unrelated*: barista, retail manager, hospitality, recent graduate, customer success rep, recruiter, even a former teacher
- Often the first OM hire at the company — you'll see "Office Manager" as a brand-new title at a 60–100 person startup
- Bio sometimes still mentions the previous identity ("former barista turned office wizard")
- Posts are enthusiastic, sometimes self-deprecating ("learning so much in my new role!")
- Connections under 500, growing fast as she builds her professional network
- May have recently joined an OM Slack community and be asking entry-level questions in it

**Email signals:**
- Apologetic and over-explaining ("Sorry for the delay! Things have been so crazy here, hope this isn't too much info…")
- Will admit when she doesn't know something
- Asks "what do other companies usually do?" — looking for benchmarks because she has none
- Long emails when short would do
- Sometimes signs off with "thanks so much!!" with multiple exclamation marks

**Social signals:**
- Personal Instagram/Twitter usually visible — she hasn't fully professionalized her digital presence yet
- May post about the new job, the office, team events with genuine enthusiasm
- Often follows OM thought leaders and "first 90 days" content creators

### What she actually wants

She wants someone to tell her the right answer. She is overwhelmed, doesn't know what "good" looks like in her role, and is terrified of making a visible mistake in her first year. She is not looking for the cheapest vendor or the most strategic vendor — she's looking for the *most reassuring* vendor. She wants to be able to say to her boss "this is the standard at companies our size" and have her boss nod. She is the highest-trust, highest-loyalty subtype if you win her — and the most likely to ghost if you make her feel stupid.

### How she communicates

- Warm, eager, occasionally too eager
- Asks lots of questions, including ones she "should" already know
- Grateful for any guidance, sometimes embarrassingly so
- Will tell you about her week, her weekend, her boss — there's a real human in the email and she isn't yet good at hiding it

### How to respond to her

**Subject line:** *"Quick guide: catering at 80–150 person companies (no pitch)"* or *"For new Office Managers — 3 things I'd want to know"*

**First message structure:**
1. Lead with empathy — name the fact that the OM role is hard and underappreciated
2. Offer education before offering product — share a one-pager titled something like "What good catering looks like at 100-person tech companies"
3. Position CBC as a calm, reliable answer to her chaos
4. CTA: a no-pressure call where you "walk her through how other companies in her stage handle the catering program"

**Avoid:**
- Making her feel like she should already know things
- Pricing-first framing — she doesn't have benchmarks yet, so price means nothing to her
- Treating her like an expert evaluator — she isn't yet
- Being condescending in the empathy — she's smart, she's just new

**Cadence:** Friendly touches every 2 weeks for the first month, then monthly. Send her useful content (an industry benchmark, a checklist) as the touch — not just "checking in." She will read it.

**Trigger to escalate:** When she asks an open-ended question like "what do you recommend for a 100-person all-hands?" — that's a buy signal disguised as a question. Respond with both a recommendation *and* an offer to handle it for her.

**The long-term play:** If you win her early in her career, she will take you with her to her next job — and her next, and her next. The Accidental OM today is the Veteran Operator in 5 years. This is the highest career-LTV subtype.

## Subtype D · The Workplace Experience Builder

### Spot her in 10 seconds

**LinkedIn signals:**
- Title is rarely "Office Manager" — it's "Workplace Experience Manager," "Workplace Coordinator," "Head of Workplace," or "Director of Workplace Experience"
- Career arc may include WeWork, Industrious, Convene, an experience-design agency, or a tier-1 tech company workplace team (Google, Airbnb, Stripe, Asana)
- Active LinkedIn presence: posts about workplace design, hybrid work, "the future of the office," employee experience metrics
- Follows and engages with workplace thought leaders (Leesman, WORKTECH, Hello Brio)
- Bio language uses words like "design," "intentional," "experience," "ecosystem"
- Speaks at conferences or panels (or aspires to)
- Connections 1500+, often including architects, interior designers, real estate brokers

**Email signals:**
- Articulate, sometimes academic — talks about "the experience layer" and "intentional moments"
- References frameworks (Maslow's hierarchy of workplace needs, the "third place" concept)
- Curious about your *brand story* and *design philosophy* — wants to know what you stand for, not just what you sell
- Will ask about packaging design, presentation aesthetics, sustainability sourcing
- Slower to respond than the Veteran but more thoughtful when she does

**Social signals:**
- Active on LinkedIn, sometimes Twitter/X
- Personal Instagram may be aesthetic — design references, travel, food photography
- Pinterest sometimes visible with workplace inspiration boards

### What she actually wants

She wants the catering to be *part of a designed workplace experience*, not a vendor relationship. She thinks of food as a touchpoint in the broader employee experience — like the lobby plants, the music in the kitchen, the welcome ritual for new hires. She wants vendors who think the same way. She is also building her personal brand as a workplace experience leader and is constantly looking for vendors and ideas she can talk about, share, and credit. She is the second-highest revenue subtype after the OM/HR Hybrid because her budgets are bigger and her programs are more ambitious.

### How she communicates

- Articulate, thoughtful, sometimes a little jargon-heavy
- Genuinely interested in the *why* behind your business
- Will ask probing questions about your chefs, your sourcing, your sustainability practices
- Wants a vendor she can name-check in her own LinkedIn posts and conference talks

### How to respond to her

**Subject line:** *"Re: your post on intentional workplace moments"* or *"A different way to think about office food programs"*

**First message structure:**
1. Reference something specific she has written or shared on LinkedIn
2. Position CBC as a *philosophy*, not a vendor — talk about chef ownership, multi-cuisine representation, the deliberate choice of independent chefs over aggregator catering
3. Offer her something she can use in her own thought leadership — a data point, a framework, a story
4. CTA: a "concept conversation" — not a sales call — about how food fits into workplace experience design

**Avoid:**
- Pure operational language — she'll read it as transactional
- Stock food photography — she has high visual standards and notices
- Calling it "catering" too much — she thinks of it as "the food experience layer"
- Talking about price first — she'll get there but it's not her opening question

**Cadence:** Weekly LinkedIn engagement (likes, thoughtful comments) for 4–6 weeks before the first DM. Then a substantive DM, then patient nurture. Treat her like a Marcus-lite in cadence.

**Trigger to escalate:** When she engages with one of your LinkedIn posts or shares anything about workplace food, send a thoughtful DM within 24 hours referencing the engagement.

## Subtype E · The OM/HR Hybrid

### Spot her in 10 seconds

**LinkedIn signals:**
- Title is doubled up: "Office Manager & HR Coordinator," "People & Operations Manager," "People Ops & Workplace Lead"
- Company is usually 30–100 people — small enough that one person owns both functions
- Career background often includes recruiting, HR coordination, or people ops in addition to OM work
- Posts about both operational topics (workplace, vendors, events) AND people topics (hiring, culture, benefits)
- May have an SHRM certification or be working toward one
- Connections include both OM peers and HR/People peers

**Email signals:**
- Mixes operational and strategic language in the same message
- Will reference both "the team's experience" and "logistics" in one breath
- Asks about both reliability AND dietary inclusion / cultural representation
- Cares about vendor values and labor practices — will sometimes ask about how chefs are paid

**Social signals:**
- LinkedIn-active, often shares People Ops content
- May follow both OM influencers and People Ops influencers

### What she actually wants

She wants a vendor who understands that catering is *both* an operational reliability question *and* an employee experience question. She is essentially a Marcus-in-training operating with Sarah's resources. She has Marcus's instincts — culture, retention, employee experience — but Sarah's day-to-day reality of running everything herself with no team. The vendor that wins her is the one that respects both halves of her job and doesn't reduce her to one or the other.

### How she communicates

- Thoughtful, slightly more strategic than pure-Sarah communication
- Will ask values-based questions (chef compensation, sustainability, dietary inclusion) earlier in the conversation than other subtypes
- Treats vendor selection as a small but meaningful values decision

### How to respond to her

**Subject line:** *"For People + Workplace leads at growing companies"* or *"Catering as a culture and ops decision"*

**First message structure:**
1. Acknowledge the dual nature of her role explicitly
2. Lead with a hybrid hook: reliability + culture in the same breath ("the food shows up on time AND tells the right story about your team")
3. Talk about chefs as people (independent operators, multi-cuisine, fair revenue split with CBC) — she will care about this
4. Mention dietary labeling as both a logistics and an inclusion feature
5. CTA: a tasting framed as "both a vendor evaluation and a small culture moment for your team"

**Avoid:**
- Reducing her to either pure ops OR pure people
- Marcus-grade ROI frameworks (too corporate for her stage)
- Sarah-grade pure logistics (too transactional for her)

**Cadence:** Bi-weekly touches mixing operational content and culture content. She'll engage with both, in different moods.

**Trigger to escalate:** When she asks about chef sourcing, dietary inclusion philosophy, or vendor values — that's a buy signal. Respond with substantive answers, not deflections.

# Part 2 · The 60-Second Identification Flowchart

When you land on a new prospect, run this in order. Stop at the first definitive answer.

```
START → Look at her LinkedIn title

│
├─ Title contains "EA," "Executive Assistant," or "Chief of Staff"
│  → SUBTYPE B (Promoted EA)
│
├─ Title contains "Workplace Experience," "Workplace Lead,"
│  "Head of Workplace," or "Director of Workplace"
│  → SUBTYPE D (Workplace Experience Builder)
│
├─ Title contains "People," "HR," or doubled with "Office Manager"
│  (e.g., "People Ops & Office Manager")
│  → SUBTYPE E (OM/HR Hybrid)
│
├─ Title is plain "Office Manager" → check tenure next
│   │
│   ├─ Less than 18 months in role AND previous role unrelated
│   │  → SUBTYPE C (Accidental OM)
│   │
│   ├─ 5+ years across 2+ OM roles with progression
│   │  → SUBTYPE A (Veteran Operator)
│   │
│   └─ 18 months – 5 years, single company → check posts
│       │
│       ├─ Posts thoughtfully about workplace topics
│       │  → SUBTYPE D-leaning (treat as Workplace Experience Builder)
│       │
│       ├─ Almost no LinkedIn activity, terse profile
│       │  → SUBTYPE A-leaning (treat as Veteran Operator)
│       │
│       └─ Enthusiastic posts, growing network, mid-career energy
│          → SUBTYPE C-leaning (treat as Accidental OM, recently graduated)
│
└─ Title is unclear or non-standard
   → Default to SUBTYPE A handling (terse, operational, COI-ready)
   → Adjust after first reply tells you more
```

## The 3-question email confirmation

After your first email reply, you can confirm the subtype with these signals from her response:

| Her reply contains… | Subtype confirmation |
|---|---|
| "COI," "W-9," "dock," "loading," "delivery window" | Veteran Operator |
| "Our CEO," "for our leadership team," "our exec" | Promoted EA |
| "I'm new to this," "what do other companies do," "any advice" | Accidental OM |
| "Experience," "intentional," "design," "story," "philosophy" | Workplace Experience Builder |
| "Culture," "inclusion," "values," "team experience," "people" | OM/HR Hybrid |

If her reply contains signals from two columns, treat her as the more strategic one (priority order: D > E > B > A > C). The more strategic framing always lands well; the operational framing can feel reductive.

# Part 4 · Response Calibration Cheat Sheet

When you sit down to respond to a prospect, run through this in 30 seconds before drafting:

| Question | Use the answer to calibrate… |
|---|---|
| What subtype is she? | The whole tone of the message |
| What was the emotional energy of her last message? | Match it (terse → terse, warm → warm) |
| Is there a buy signal in her last message? | Whether to escalate or stay patient |
| What does she care about that I haven't yet addressed? | The hook of this specific message |
| What's the single thing I want her to do next? | The CTA — exactly one |
| Have I sent her anything generic in past touches? | Apologize implicitly by being extra specific now |
| Would she screenshot this message and forward it to a peer? | The proof of whether the message is good enough to send |

Last question is the bar. If she wouldn't share it with another OM, it's not sharp enough yet.

# Part 5 · Common identification mistakes to avoid

1. **Don't classify by company size alone.** A 200-person Series C might have any of the five subtypes. The person, not the company, determines the subtype.

2. **Don't over-weight the title.** Some Workplace Experience Builders have plain "Office Manager" titles because their company hasn't caught up to the role evolution. Read the posts and recommendations before classifying.

3. **Don't classify on first email content alone.** People write differently when they're stressed, hurried, or replying from their phone. Cross-reference with LinkedIn before locking in.

4. **Don't assume a Veteran is a Veteran forever.** A Veteran who's burned out and looking for her next role behaves more like an Accidental OM in disposition — open, reflective, exploring. Look at recent post activity for clues.

5. **Don't forget the dual-buyer scenario.** If you're at a 150–250-person company, there may be a Marcus above her in the org chart. Check LinkedIn for the People/HR head before assuming she's the sole decision-maker. If Marcus exists, your sequence to her should include language that gives her ammunition to bring him along.

6. **Update the profile after every interaction.** The subtype classification is a hypothesis, not a verdict. If her reply contradicts your initial read, change the classification. A misclassified prospect ghosted is almost always a prospect you read wrong, not a prospect who wasn't interested.
