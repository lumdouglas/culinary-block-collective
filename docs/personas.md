## Avatar 1 · "The Overwhelmed Office Manager"

### Identity & Role

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Composite Name** | Sarah Nguyen |
| **Job Title** | Office Manager / Executive Assistant |
| **Company Type** | Series B–C tech startup, SaaS or hardware |
| **Company Size** | 75–200 employees |
| **Location** | Mountain View, Palo Alto, or San Jose |
| **Age Range** | 28–42 |
| **Budget Authority** | $1,500–$5,000 per event; recurring $3K–$8K/month |
| **Reports To** | VP of Operations or COO |
| **LinkedIn Title** | Office Manager at [Company] \| Culture & Operations |

### A Day in Sarah's Life

Sarah manages everything nobody else owns. Before 9 AM she's already handling a broken HVAC, a guest wifi outage, and a Slack from the CEO asking what the all-hands lunch plan is — for next Tuesday. Catering isn't her only job. It's one of 40. She values vendors who make her look competent without requiring hand-holding.

She books catering 6–12 times per year for recurring events (monthly all-hands, weekly team lunches for engineering) plus ad hoc needs (client visits, product launch celebrations, onboarding days). She's building a short list of trusted vendors she rotates between — and she's actively looking for a reliable replacement after her last caterer delivered the wrong order to a 120-person all-hands.

### Goals & Motivations

- Look like the hero: catering that lands on time, impresses leadership, and generates zero complaints
- Build a reliable vendor list she can trust without babysitting every order
- Simplify the ordering process — the fewer emails and calls to confirm, the better
- Accommodate her increasingly diverse workforce: vegan, halal-adjacent, nut allergies, gluten-free
- Stay within budget without getting surprised by hidden delivery or service fees
- Get recurring orders on autopilot so she can focus on higher-priority work

### Event Types & Tier Preference

Sarah books for both everyday and high-stakes events. The service tier she needs — and the message that lands — differs by event type.

| Event Type | Typical Sarah Scenario | Recommended Tier |
|---|---|---|
| Weekly/bi-weekly team lunch | Recurring engineering or ops team meals | Tier 1 — Premium Drop-Off |
| Monthly all-hands | 100–200 employees, company-wide | Tier 1 (cost-conscious) or Tier 2 (if CEO is in the room) |
| Client visit / VIP lunch | Small group (30–50), impression matters | Tier 2 — Attended Service |
| Onboarding day | New hire cohort meals | Tier 1 |
| Product launch / celebration | Company milestone, visible to leadership | Tier 2 — Attended Service |

**Outreach messaging by event type:**
- **Tier 1 lead (recurring/team meals):** Value and convenience — *"Lock in weekly catering on autopilot. Fixed per-head pricing, rotating menus, zero admin week to week."*
- **Tier 2 lead (events that matter):** Impression and experience — *"When the CEO or a client is in the room, the food is part of the statement. Our attended service handles setup, the spread, and cleanup — you just show up."*

---

### Pain Points & Frustrations

- Previous caterers delivered generic menus — pizza and sandwiches again — and teams stopped being excited about catered meals
- Dietary labeling failures have caused real incidents: a team member with a nut allergy received unlabeled food
- Last-minute scrambles when preferred vendors cancel or go unavailable
- Hidden fees on invoices — service charges, delivery surcharges — blow her budget and require awkward expense reapprovals
- Coordinating across multiple vendors for a single event (food + drinks) wastes time
- No visibility into delivery status — she can't tell leadership when food will arrive
- Short-notice orders are a regular reality — the CEO announces an all-hands Tuesday morning for Thursday; she needs a caterer who can turn that around in 48–72 hours without chaos
- After a vendor fails her, every new vendor's promises sound the same — she has a trust deficit that only concrete guarantees, not sales language, can overcome

### Discovery & Buying Behavior

| Channel | Details |
|---|---|
| **Google Searches** | "corporate catering Mountain View" · "office lunch catering San Jose" · "catering for 150 people Bay Area" |
| **Discovery** | LinkedIn vendor recommendations in Office Managers groups · Google Reviews · Peer referrals |
| **Decision Triggers** | A peer recommends a vendor · Previous vendor fails · Company headcount crosses 100+ |
| **Vetting Process** | Checks Google Reviews, looks at menu variety, asks for a sample quote, evaluates response speed |

### What CBC Offers Sarah

- ✓ Multiple specialized cuisines (Chinese, sushi/Japanese, Mexican fusion, American, and more) in one order — no more generic menus, team excitement is back
- ✓ Every item individually labeled (vegan, GF, nut-free) — dietary incident risk eliminated
- ✓ On-time delivery guarantee — if food isn't staged and ready for your team, next order is 10% off
- ✓ Transparent pricing — no hidden fees, no invoice surprises
- ✓ Recurring account setup — weekly order locked in, fixed pricing, zero admin every week
- ✓ Single point of contact — one phone call, one text, one human who knows her account

### Objections & How to Handle Them

**"We already have a caterer"**
> Ask: 'Are you happy with their menu variety and dietary labeling?' Most aren't. Offer a free tasting as a no-risk comparison.

**"Your price is higher than our current vendor"**
> Reframe: 'Our price includes delivery, setup, and full dietary labeling — what's included in their quote?' Almost always uncovers hidden fees on their end.

**"I need to get approval"**
> Provide a one-pager and a sample quote formatted for easy internal forwarding. Offer to jump on a 10-min call with their manager.

**"We're not ready to commit to a contract"**
> Offer a single-event trial with the 10% first-order discount. No commitment required.

**"You sound like every other vendor who promised reliability — how are you actually different?"**
> Don't respond with more claims. Respond with proof: the delivery guarantee is written into every order (late = food not staged and ready for your team = 10% off your next order), there's a named single point of contact who knows her account, and she can text the same number at 8am the morning of an event. Offer a reference call with a client who had a vendor failure before switching to CBC.

**"We sometimes need catering on 48 hours notice — can you actually do that?"**
> Yes — for orders under 150 guests, CBC's minimum booking window is 48 hours. That's the answer. Confirm it directly and don't hedge.

### Best Outreach Approach

**Channel Priority: LinkedIn DM → Email → Phone**

Lead with pain, not product. The hook that converts:
> *"You've heard 'reliable' before. We back ours with a guarantee: if your food isn't staged and ready for your team, you get 10% off your next order — in writing, every order."*

- **Email 1:** Reliability + dietary labeling promise — her two biggest fears
- **Follow-up:** Real menu examples (Chinese dim sum spread, sushi spread) to spark team excitement
- **Offer:** Free chef tasting — low-risk, high-impact
- **Close:** Recurring account proposal — "one less thing to manage every week"

### Revenue Potential

| Revenue Type | Value |
|---|---|
| Single Event | $4,500–$6,000 (avg 150 guests × $30–$40/person + add-ons) |
| Annual One-Time Events | 6–10 events/year = $18K–$60K annually |
| Recurring Contract | $3,000–$6,000/month = $36K–$72K/year |
| Referral Value | Sarah knows 3–6 other OMs — 1 referral = $15K–$40K new revenue |
| Upsell Potential | Juice & Boba add-ons, premium packaging, Chef's Table for VIP events |

## Avatar 2 · "The Culture-Driven HR Director"

### Identity & Role

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Composite Name** | Marcus Chen |
| **Job Title** | HR Director / Head of People / VP People Operations |
| **Company Type** | Mid-size tech company, Series C–D or post-IPO |
| **Company Size** | 150–300 employees |
| **Location** | San Jose, Sunnyvale, or Santa Clara |
| **Age Range** | 34–50 |
| **Budget Authority** | $5,000–$15,000 per quarter for employee experience programs |
| **Reports To** | COO or CEO directly |
| **LinkedIn Title** | Head of People at [Company] \| Employee Experience \| Culture Builder |

### A Day in Marcus's Life

Marcus owns employee experience from end to end: hiring, retention, culture, wellness, and benefits. In 2025, food is a retention strategy. He knows the data — companies with strong food programs see measurably lower attrition, and his CEO is asking him to justify the perks budget before renewals in Q3.

Catering is a line item in Marcus's People Ops budget, not an administrative task. He's not ordering lunch — he's building culture. He evaluates vendors on three things: does the food represent the diversity of our team, does it support wellness goals, and can this vendor scale with us as we grow from 200 to 400 people?

Marcus is also the internal champion who will sell this vendor to finance. He needs numbers — cost per head, projected quarterly spend, ROI narrative — not just a menu PDF.

### Goals & Motivations

- Improve employee retention and satisfaction scores — food is a measurable lever
- Demonstrate ROI on the perks budget to CFO and CEO before Q3 budget review
- Build a recurring catering program that runs on autopilot — not a manual weekly task
- Represent the cultural diversity of a tech workforce (20+ nationalities at his company)
- Align food program with ESG / sustainability commitments the company has made publicly
- Reduce the administrative burden on his team — fewer vendors, simpler processes

### Pain Points & Frustrations

- Generic catering options (pizza, sandwiches) generate low team satisfaction scores — employees comment on this in internal surveys
- Current vendors can't scale: great at 80 people, fall apart at 200+
- No sustainability story — current vendor uses styrofoam packaging, which conflicts with the company's published ESG targets
- Dietary options are an afterthought — a third of his team is vegan, vegetarian, or has documented allergies
- Multiple vendors for different needs creates admin complexity and inconsistent quality
- Can't get reliable per-head cost data for budget forecasting — invoices vary too much
- At a company with 20+ nationalities, an all-American menu (pizza, sandwiches, BBQ) sends a visible signal about whose culture gets centered — Marcus sees this as a DEI failure, not just a food quality issue
- Quality is inconsistent with aggregator-style caterers — they're a logistics company, not a culinary one; what arrives week to week depends on whoever was available, not a chef who owns their craft

### Discovery & Buying Behavior

| Channel | Details |
|---|---|
| **Google Searches** | "corporate catering contract bay area" · "office lunch catering program silicon valley" · "sustainable catering tech company" |
| **Discovery** | HR peer networks (HRBPs, Lattice community) · LinkedIn articles · People Ops Slack groups |
| **Decision Triggers** | Low eNPS score on food satisfaction · Company hits 150+ headcount · ESG audit reveals vendor gap · New CFO asks for budget justification |
| **Vetting Process** | Requests RFP or formal proposal · Needs pricing model for quarterly budget planning · May involve procurement for contracts over $10K/quarter |

### What CBC Offers Marcus

- ✓ Multiple specialized cuisines spanning Asian, Latin, American, and fusion traditions = authentic representation of a diverse workforce's food culture
- ✓ BPI-certified compostable packaging + carbon offset option = ESG story Marcus can put in the quarterly report
- ✓ Dietary labeling on every item = wellness program alignment, zero allergy incidents
- ✓ Recurring contract pricing with predictable per-head costs = clean budget line item for finance
- ✓ Scales 100–300+ guests — grows with the company through Series D and beyond
- ✓ Single vendor for all event types: weekly lunches, all-hands, client dinners, product launches

### Objections & How to Handle Them

**"We need to go through procurement"**
> Provide a formal proposal with per-head pricing, quarterly projections, and vendor credentials. Offer a reference call with an existing client.

**"Our current vendor is under contract"**
> Ask when the contract renews. Schedule a tasting 60 days before renewal. Stay warm with monthly content touches.

**"CBC seems like a startup — are you professional and scaled enough for an enterprise contract?"**
> Lead with credentials, not enthusiasm: every chef carries $1M product and general liability insurance, CBC carries its own general liability, contractor agreements are in place, food safety logs are maintained and available on request, and all orders are tracked in HubSpot with written delivery confirmation. The on-time guarantee has financial consequences — we don't just promise reliability, we bet money on it. Offer to share insurance certificates and a sample contract.

**"What happens if one of your chefs cancels a recurring order we're counting on?"**
> CBC takes ownership of continuity. Every chef is required to give 7 days written notice for any changes to availability, and a $250 cancellation fee applies within 7 days of a confirmed order. CBC maintains a backup option for every active cuisine type. If something goes wrong, you'll hear from CBC before you're affected — never after.

**"We need more than 300 guests for some events"**
> The 300-person cap is intentional — it's the threshold where CBC's individual dietary labeling and quality standards can be fully maintained. For annual or milestone events over 300, that's a specific conversation worth having about how to structure it; we'd rather be honest about our ceiling than overpromise. The 90%+ of recurring weekly and monthly events fall well within range.

**"Can you handle weekly volume reliably?"**
> Lead with the on-time delivery guarantee and the HubSpot order tracking system. Offer a 4-week pilot with no long-term commitment.

**"I need to justify this to the CFO"**
> ROI framing: avg tech catering spend of $18/person vs. cost of one bad hire ($15K+). Food scores on eNPS surveys are measurable retention data.

### Best Outreach Approach

**Channel Priority: LinkedIn Content → LinkedIn DM → Formal Email Proposal**

Marcus is a thought leader consumer. He respects vendors who understand the People Ops function. The hook that converts:
> *"Your catering vendor is a culture statement. Does yours reflect the diversity of your team?"*

- **LinkedIn:** Publish article — 'How Corporate Catering Affects Employee Retention Scores' — Marcus engages with this before a DM lands
- **DM:** Reference a specific company initiative (ESG report, headcount growth, culture award) to show genuine research
- **Email:** Lead with sustainability angle (BPI packaging, carbon offset) and recurring program ROI narrative
- **Offer:** Free tasting framed as a team culture experience, not a sales demo
- **Proposal:** Formal catering program proposal with quarterly cost projections and 4-week pilot structure
- **Close:** Recurring contract with 90-day review clause — reduces commitment risk

### Revenue Potential

| Revenue Type | Value |
|---|---|
| Single Event | $7,500–$10,000 (avg 250 guests × $30–$40/person + premium add-ons) |
| Weekly Recurring Contract | $5,000–$12,000/month = $60K–$144K/year |
| Quarterly Program Value | $15K–$35K per quarter including all event types |
| Referral Value | 1 referral to a peer at a 200-person tech company = $60K+ annual contract |
| Upsell Potential | Organic ingredient tier, premium packaging, Chef's Table, carbon offsetting as program feature |

## Avatar Comparison & Sales Strategy Guide

| Factor | Sarah — Office Manager | Marcus — HR Director |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary Fear** | Caterer fails, she looks bad to leadership | Vendor doesn't align with culture/ESG, retention suffers |
| **Core Hook** | "Reliability + zero dietary incidents" | "Culinary diversity = culture representation" |
| **Decision Speed** | Fast — reactive, often last-minute | Slow — budget cycles, procurement, approvals |
| **Budget Authority** | Manages budget, limited approval authority | Owns the People budget, higher authority |
| **Best Offer** | 10% first-order discount + free tasting | 4-week pilot program + formal recurring proposal |
| **Relationship Style** | Transactional → trust-based over time | Partnership-oriented from day one |
| **Content That Works** | Menu photos, delivery guarantees, testimonials | LinkedIn articles, ROI data, sustainability credentials |
| **Upsell Path** | Recurring contract → referral | Premium packaging, organic tier, full-program expansion |
| **Annual Revenue Ceiling** | $40K+ (recurring) | $144K+ (full program) |

### How to Tell Which Avatar You're Talking To

Ask on the first call: *"Is catering something you manage personally, or does it tie into a broader employee experience program?"*

- **"I manage it personally"** → Sarah. Lead with reliability, speed, and simplicity.
- Mentions **"culture," "retention," "perks budget," or "employee experience"** → Marcus. Lead with diversity, sustainability, and program design.
- Mentions **"procurement" or "contract approval"** → almost always Marcus. Prepare a formal proposal.
